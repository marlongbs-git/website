Key Achievements

  • Designed a 3D-printed electronics enclosure in SOLIDWORKS with PCB mounting and cable routing for humidity protection
  • Built a hydroponic piping network with 10+ valves and pumps, integrating safety controls to prevent leaks and over-pressure failures
  • Fabricated a sheet metal mounting system meeting anti-vibration and seismic safety requirements
  • Constructed an aluminum extrusion frame and gutter assembly forming the primary structural support for the grow system
  • Developed dosing system model and documentation for future implementation

WLF Hydroponics is a student-led project within a sustainable energy school focused on building an automated indoor garden system. As part of a three-person team, each member carried significant responsibility across design, fabrication, and sourcing. Starting from rough initial sketches, the team had to develop the full system from the ground up with no established dimensions, materials plan, or construction approach. I began by modeling the designated room in our engineering building to map out the available space, including placement for a plant nursery, reservoir containers, and emergency drain access. Piping design followed using SolidWorks built-in libraries to lay out connectors, elbows, and pipe lengths, along with producing drawings for construction reference. With limited funding and a sustainability mandate, component sourcing required careful decision matrices and coordination with the finance team to validate every design choice while prioritizing local suppliers.

Much of the hardware was prototyped through 3D printing, requiring detailed modeling of all electronic components and sensors to ensure proper fit within enclosures. The most physically demanding challenge came during metal fabrication, where an 11-gauge sheet metal bracket needed to be bent without a press brake. Working alongside the machinist for several hours, we produced a bracket that met the seismic safety requirements set by SFU's Faculty of Applied Science. The project reinforced skills in CAD modeling, piping design, prototyping, and hands-on fabrication under real constraints. The smaller team structure demanded greater individual ownership and pushed practical problem-solving in ways larger teams often don't.
