SFU Team Phantom Formula SAE Electric

Team Phantom is SFU's Formula SAE Electric team, bringing together engineering students from multiple disciplines to design and build a race car compliant with rigorous FSAE safety standards.

As part of the Vehicle Dynamics team, I focused on driver safety systems, including seat positioning, bracket design, and harness mounting tabs, ensuring full compliance with FSAE regulations.

Key Achievements

  • Designed and manufactured custom seat brackets accommodating drivers from 152–187 cm and 49–102 kg, meeting FSAE ergonomic and safety standards
  • Simulated and optimized harness mounting tabs in ANSYS Mechanical to achieve a 15 kN pullout load requirement with verified safety factor
  • Developed a safety harness test plan and fixture designs to validate FEA results through physical load testing
  • Managed and organized assemblies of 80+ components in SOLIDWORKS, maintaining model integrity across design iterations

Team Phantom was one of my first large-scale design teams, where I contributed to the safety systems and was responsible for fitting a legacy seat into a newly constructed frame. The core difficulty was designing around pre-existing components under tight space constraints while maintaining sufficient safety factors for all parts. The process began by recreating fitment models from previous years to understand the existing envelope, then redesigning the seat bracket to fit within the chassis geometry.

Having recently completed a Mechanical Design and Finite Element Analysis course, I applied those principles directly to this work. The simulations followed proper FEA methodology, including consistent polygon geometry throughout the mesh, minimizing internal pyramids, and reducing skewness. The same approach was applied to the harnessing tabs, where bearing loads were applied and validated against the load requirements specified in the competition ruleset. This project was a meaningful opportunity to apply classroom theory to a real engineering problem with genuine consequences. Knowing the difference between running a simulation and running one where the physics actually makes sense was a lesson that carried forward into every project since.
