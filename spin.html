Solar Cell Testing Fixture

Developed as part of the Energy Harvesting Materials Course, this 2-axis rotating platform characterizes organic solar cell performance by measuring voltage and current output across multiple tilt angles. The system integrated a stepper motor, a custom PCB with copper contacts, an Arduino microcontroller, a motor driver, and rotational mounting platforms.

My role focused on designing the mechanical fixture that interfaces the stepper motor to the solar cell, along with electronic assembly and hardware debugging.

Key Achievements

  • Designed and 3D-printed a mechanical fixture enabling full 180° pitch solar cell rotation for angular characterization
  • Assembled and debugged a custom PCB with a copper contact array for reliable electrical interfacing
  • Acquired and analyzed I-V curve data using an Ossila Source Measure Unit, demonstrating a bell-curve relationship between pitch angle and power conversion efficiency

Project SPIN is an automated rotation platform designed to characterize solar cell performance across varying angles of incident light. The system controls polar and azimuthal angles through a motorized fixture built with 3D-printed components and a spring-loaded probe PCB, synchronized with an Ossila Source Measure Unit to acquire I-V curves at defined positions and produce repeatable efficiency measurements under realistic illumination conditions.

The platform went through significant iteration throughout the build. Initial tolerances were too tight due to print speed settings, requiring resizing of the base component and adjustments to print orientation and nozzle diameter to achieve accurate fits. The substrate holder was the most sensitive assembly, with features refined to the tenth of a millimeter and reprinted repeatedly to ensure flush contact. All fasteners were designed to be fully recessed to eliminate cast shadows during rotation, which was critical to measurement accuracy. Hardware debugging covered cable continuity checks and voltage verification across the motor, power supply, and driver to confirm correct operation throughout the system. The result was a functional piece of lab equipment with full documentation, delivered within a class timeline. The project reinforced how much can be achieved through rapid iteration and highlighted the impact that print settings, orientation, and tolerance planning have on the quality of functional prototypes.
